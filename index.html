Understanding Your Feelings

Feelings often reflect your needs. Learn how to recognise what’s going on inside.

The Overwhelmed Mind

Stress can feel like:

  • Tension in your shoulders or jaw
  • Difficulty focusing or feeling scattered
  • Feeling “on alert” even when nothing is wrong
  • Everything feels urgent

The Insight: Your body is trying to protect you by staying ready for action, even when you need rest.

The Racing Heart

Anxiety can feel like:

  • A fast or fluttery heartbeat
  • Restlessness or trouble sitting still
  • Worrying about “what ifs”
  • Feeling on edge or easily startled

The Insight: Your brain is preparing for danger, even when you're safe—a natural response that can become overwhelming.

The Exhausted Body

Burnout can feel like:

  • Constant tiredness that rest doesn’t fix
  • Feeling numb or disconnected
  • Losing interest in things you used to enjoy
  • Feeling drained by even small tasks

The Insight: Your energy reserves are depleted from prolonged stress, signaling a need for deep rest and recovery.

The Heavy Feeling

Low mood can feel like:

  • Feeling sad or empty
  • Not enjoying your usual activities
  • Changes to your sleep or appetite
  • Thoughts of hopelessness or worthlessness

The Insight: If you've felt this way for two weeks or more it could be a sign of depression, and you may want to seek support from your GP.

The Nature Connection

Six ways to use the natural world as a support system for your mind.

Habit
The 120-Minute Dose

Aim for a total of two hours in nature each week. Whether it’s one long immersion or 15-minute daily "doses" in a local park.

The Why: This threshold is scientifically linked to a cumulative boost in psychological well-being.
Kindness
Active "Giving"

Engage in small acts like hanging a bird feeder or joining a local clean-up. Shift from passive observer to active protector.

The Why: Giving back provides purpose and helps transform "eco-anxiety" into positive action.
Focus
Soft Fascination

Watch effortless movements: clouds drifting or ripples on water. Avoid your phone and let your gaze wander without analyzing.

The Why: These patterns allow your brain’s cognitive resources to naturally replenish and restore focus.
Nurture
The Joy of Tending

Nurture something living, like a window box or houseplant. If frustrated, try "destructive" gardening like weeding or digging.

The Why: It provides a tangible sense of achievement and a healthy vent for pent-up tension.
Social
Side-by-Side Talk

Invite someone for a "walk and talk." Focus on the path ahead rather than sitting face-to-face in a room.

The Why: Moving side-by-side reduces the pressure of eye contact, making it easier to share difficult feelings.
Accessibility
The Wild Indoors

Create a "nature nook" with found objects like stones, rain sounds, or a chair positioned with a clear view of the sky.

The Why: Seeing and hearing nature triggers a relaxation response similar to being physically outside.
